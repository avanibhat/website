What I Learned About Low-Dose Pediatric PET/CT and AI-Based Reconstruction

Medical imaging sits at an interesting intersection of physics, biology, and machine learning. While working on author and literature metadata in my current work, I came across an interesting paper focused on low-dose pediatric PET/CT, which I found fascinating and that is how I went into this deep hole to understand the problem and the question people I trying to solve when two things like imaging and AI come together.

This post summarizes the key ideas and insights I learned along the way.

Why Low-Dose Pediatric PET Is a Hard Problem

PET/CT imaging is essential for diagnosing and monitoring cancer in children. However, children are significantly more radiosensitive than adults and often require repeated scans over time. This creates a fundamental tension:

  • Lower dose is safer
  • Higher dose is more informative

In pediatric settings, additional constraints make things harder:

  • Shorter scan times due to motion and sedation limits
  • Smaller body size
  • Reduced photon counts during acquisition

Shorter scans mean fewer detected photons, which directly leads to high noise in PET images. This noise is not just a visual problem — it degrades:

  • Quantitative accuracy (e.g. SUV measurements)
  • Small lesion detectability
  • Clinical reliability of follow-up scans

Any AI solution must therefore denoise PET images without destroying quantitative meaning.

Thinking About Denoising at the Right Level

One key insight is that PET denoising is not a single problem — it can be approached at multiple levels:

  • Image-level denoising
    Post-reconstruction filtering or neural networks operating on PET images directly.
  • Cross-modal denoising (PET–CT fusion)
    Using anatomical information from CT to guide PET reconstruction.
  • Physics-aware reconstruction
    Incorporating acquisition physics and long-range spatial consistency into the model.

Most traditional approaches focus heavily on the first level. Recent research suggests that the real gains come from combining all three.

PET–CT Fusion: Learning Jointly, Not Sequentially

CT images provide strong, high-resolution anatomical priors. PET images, on the other hand, encode metabolic activity but are noisy at low dose.

A key conceptual shift is this:

PET and CT should be learned jointly, not treated as separate steps.

In a PET–CT fusion framework:

  • PET and CT are processed by separate encoders
  • Their features are combined through a fusion block
  • The model learns where CT information is useful and where PET should dominate

Fusion can be implemented through:

  • Feature concatenation
  • Attention mechanisms
  • Gating strategies

This allows CT to guide PET denoising specifically in low-count regions, without hallucinating activity where none exists.

The Importance of Long-Range Context in Whole-Body PET

Whole-body PET images exhibit long-range spatial dependencies:

  • Activity in one region is often correlated with distant regions
  • Noise patterns are not purely local

Standard CNNs struggle with this because their receptive fields are limited.

This is where state-space models (such as Mamba) become interesting. They offer:

  • Efficient modeling of long-range dependencies
  • Better global consistency
  • A natural way to enforce coherence across the entire body

Combining these models with physics-informed constraints helps preserve anatomical and quantitative plausibility.

Dose-Aware Diffusion Models: Adapting to Noise Levels

Another promising idea is dose-conditioned diffusion modeling.

Diffusion models iteratively remove noise through a learned reverse process. For PET:

  • Low-dose scans correspond to higher noise
  • Different dose levels require different denoising behavior

By conditioning the diffusion process on dose or count level, the model can:

  • Adapt to varying noise regimes
  • Avoid over-denoising high-quality scans
  • Maintain consistency across longitudinal studies

This is particularly important in pediatrics, where dose varies significantly between patients and follow-ups.

Final Thoughts

What stood out most to me is that low-dose pediatric PET is not a "denoising problem" — it is a trust problem.

Any AI system must:

  • Preserve quantitative accuracy
  • Respect physical constraints
  • Be interpretable enough for clinical adoption

This makes the field challenging, but also deeply rewarding. It sits at a point where careful modeling choices matter as much as raw performance, and where interdisciplinary thinking is essential.
